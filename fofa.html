Publication Design

Material Trace

———designed for Fofa Gallery

Material Trace
was designed for the annual publication for the FOFA Undergraduate Student Exhibition (USE) in 2019. USE is an annual exhibition held at Concordia’s FOFA Gallery to celebrate the talents of numerous creatives: visual artists, writers, designers, and contemporary dancers. This interdisciplinary initiative culminates in an exhibition, publication, and performance event, and combines the efforts of many students, staff, and faculty members. Writers from the Department of Art History create essays about the exhibited artworks, which are featured in a catalogue created by design students.

The publication was a joint effort between myself and four other designers (Lindsey Carter, Andi Hernandez, Carolina Ovalle, and Gavin Park). We were commissioned to design, print, and deliver the exhibition catalogue in time for the undergraduate exhibition. We were given full creative freedom, the only requirements were to equitably represent the 12 artworks and their artists, the corresponding essays, and their authors in a bilingual printed archive.

concordia.ca

Through multiple rounds of research and brainstorming, we came to the decision to approach the exhibition catalogue from the perspective of material research and archival practices. We were given the opportunity to name that year’s exhibition and we chose the title Material Trace to express the prevalent tendency in the selected artworks to give a physical form to ideas by collecting, recording, logging, documenting and archiving.

Keeping with this theme of archive and collection, we chose beige and off-white paper stocks to give an aged effect, reminiscent of old papers stored in a filing cabinet. Since the catalogue would be printed in both English and French, I suggested we approach this a bit differently by creating two separate catalogues and biding them together in a gatefold. The layouts were made complementary to eachother so that the unilingual catalogues could be viewed together or separately. This juxtaposition made the publication a very novel object to handle.

A handwritten element was introduced into the catalogue by bringing in signatures from the authors and artists to go along with their corresponding essay and artwork. We decided to keep to a minimal grid system throughout and incorporated lines which, in tandem with the signatures, gave the look of an old library card. These elements came together to create a nostalgic yet modern printed piece.

Along with designing the catalogue in collaboration with my team, I was also responsible for all communications with the client and the catalogue’s featured artists and authors. I had a hand in all elements of the project, from research, brainstorming initial concepts, collecting signatures, exploring binding methods, and designing a large number of spreads in the exhibition catalogue itself. The publication really came together in a fun and interesting way and was very well received by artists, authors, students and faculty alike.
